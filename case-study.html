Anatomy of a Cloaked Tech-Support Scam Delivered via Google Ads

A step-by-step reconstruction of how an ad-cloaking operation ("kelveronia") hides a fake-Microsoft tech-support scam from ad reviewers while serving it to real victims — and how it is detected and dismantled.

Analyst:   Date: July 2026 Classification: Educational / Defensive Sample: live, captured in isolation

Abstract

This case study dissects a live malvertising campaign that used client-side cloaking to pass Google Ads review. A single URL served two entirely different pages: a benign "ShopEase" storefront to any automated reviewer, and — only after a real mouse movement — a fake "Microsoft Support" browser-locker that pressures victims into calling a fraudulent call-centre (+1-866-881-5397). The malicious page was never present in the source: it was fetched AES-encrypted from a second, Cloudflare-fronted server and rebuilt in memory as a blob: URL inside a hidden iframe, leaving no crawlable address. The operation is notable not for sophisticated code, but for a chain of cheap evasions that each defeat a specific layer of automated defence.

1Objective (the attackers' goal)

The operators run a tech-support scam: frighten a user into believing their computer is infected, then get them to phone a fake "Microsoft" number where an operator sells bogus "support" and/or obtains remote access and payment.

To feed victims to that page cheaply and at scale, they buy Google Search/Display ads. The obstacle: Google's automated review would instantly reject a fake-Microsoft page. Their core problem is therefore not technical delivery but review evasion — the ad, and its landing page, must look 100% legitimate to Google's bots while showing the scam only to humans. Every design choice below serves that single goal.

3The attack chain, step by step

0
Preparation (offline)
Stage the two-sided site. Build the benign "ShopEase" page and embed the cloaking script + two keys. Separately, AES-encrypt the fake-Microsoft page and place the ciphertext on the vault server. Register/point the ad campaign at the landing URL.
1
Delivery
Buy the click. A Google ad drives a visitor to the white page (arriving with a gclid). Google's review system has already seen — and approved — only the clean store.
2
Arming
Load & wait. The page renders the store behind a "Loading… please wait" overlay and registers a single mousemove listener ({once:true}). Nothing malicious is present yet; a source scan finds only a shop.
3
Evasion / triage
Human check. If no mouse ever moves (automated reviewer, crawler, sandbox) the page stays clean forever. A real cursor movement is treated as proof of a human and fires the payload routine.
4
Retrieval
Phone home. The script decrypts the vault URL with URL_KEY, then fetches node.apigetdata.click/data?platform=win|mac — receiving ~4.4 MB of ciphertext tailored to the victim's OS.
5
Decrypt & assemble
Rebuild in memory. The ciphertext is AES-decrypted in-browser with PASSPHRASE, converted to a blob: URL, and loaded into a hidden <iframe>. The store is hidden; the iframe is shown.
6
Execution / impact
Trap the victim. A full-screen fake "Microsoft Support" page appears (fake command prompt, autoplay scare-audio), grabs full-screen, blocks tab-close (onbeforeunload) and disables keys — pushing the victim to call +1-866-881-5397.

7Victim impact

A victim who moves their mouse sees a convincing full-screen Microsoft-branded "your computer is infected" lockscreen they cannot easily close. Believing it, they call the number and reach a scam operator, whose typical outcomes are: paying for worthless "support/antivirus," granting remote access (enabling data theft or real malware), and/or handing over card details — i.e. financial loss and potential follow-on compromise.

8Detection & remediation (blue-team)

How to detect it
  • Dynamic analysis with synthetic input: drive the page headless and dispatch a mousemove — the cloak flips and self-reveals. This single step defeats the whole gate.
  • Egress monitoring: a static "shop" that, on interaction, fetches a large blob from a different domain is the fingerprint.
  • On-device content heuristics: modern browsers (Chrome's on-device model; Edge SmartScreen; Malwarebytes Browser Guard) flag the rendered page by behaviour — forced fullscreen, keyboard/pointer lock, fake warnings — regardless of blob/encryption.
  • Why file-AV misses it: nothing is written to disk; it is pure browser-based social engineering.
How to kill it
  • Report the landing URL & payload host to Google Safe Browsing and Microsoft SmartScreen (one listing blocks it for entire browser user-bases).
  • File abuse reports: DigitalOcean (Spaces object), Cloudflare (proxied vault), the .click registrar for apigetdata.click, and the Google Ads account behind the gclid.
  • Report the phone number to carrier/FTC-style fraud channels.
  • Reality check: blocklisting kills one URL; operators rotate disposable domains faster than listings propagate, so registrar/host takedown of the infrastructure is the durable fix.

9Lessons learned

  • Cheap evasion beats expensive code. No exploit is used — just a stack of low-effort tricks, each defeating one automated layer.
  • "Encryption" without a hidden key is only obfuscation. The embedded keys made the payload fully recoverable by an analyst; it only ever slowed down passive scanners.
  • The cloak is strong only against a passive look. One synthetic mousemove plus egress inspection collapses the entire scheme — which is exactly why active, in-browser, behaviour-based detection is the effective countermeasure.
  • Ad networks are part of the attack surface. The whole design exists to defeat ad review; defence must include reviewer tooling that simulates human interaction.
