How the Scammers Did It — Step by Step

A plain, step-by-step account of how an online scam used a fake shop page and a hidden trick to slip a fake "Microsoft Support" page past Google's ad checks — and show it only to real people.

Prepared by:   Date: July 2026 Purpose: Educational study

In short

The scammers built one web link that shows two different pages. Google's checking-robots saw a harmless online shop, so they approved the ad. But a real person who clicked the ad and moved their mouse was quietly shown something else: a fake "Microsoft Support" page that locks the screen and pressures them to call a fake help number. This write-up lists, in plain steps, exactly how they set that up and made it work.

What they were trying to do (their goal)

Their goal was to scare ordinary people into calling a fake "Microsoft" phone number, where a fake agent takes their money or gets into their computer.

The problem for the scammers: to reach people cheaply they wanted to use Google ads — but Google checks every ad, and would instantly reject a fake-Microsoft page. So their real challenge was simple: look completely innocent to Google's checkers, but show the scam to real humans. Every step below is built around that one idea.

The step-by-step process — how they achieved it

1

They built a harmless-looking fake shop page

A plain, innocent online store ("ShopEase") was created and put on a free hosting service. This is the "clean face" that anyone — or any robot — sees first.

Why: it gives Google's checkers something completely innocent to look at.
2

They prepared the real scam page separately — and scrambled it

The actual fake "Microsoft Support" page was built on its own. Then it was scrambled with a password (so it looks like meaningless gibberish) and stored on a different, hidden server — not on the shop page.

Why: if it's scrambled and kept elsewhere, a quick look at the shop page shows no trace of the scam.
3

They hid a small set of instructions inside the shop page

Tucked inside the innocent shop page was a tiny piece of instructions that simply waits quietly and does nothing — until one specific thing happens (see step 6).

Why: nothing bad runs automatically, so the page looks and behaves normally at first.
4

They bought Google ads pointing to the shop page

They paid for Google ads that send clicks to the innocent shop page, to bring in a stream of real visitors.

Why: ads are a cheap, fast way to put the trap in front of lots of people.
5

Google's robots checked the ad — and saw only the shop

When Google's automatic checkers reviewed the page, the hidden instructions stayed asleep, so the robots only ever saw the harmless shop. Google approved the ad.

Why it worked: the checkers are automated and don't move a mouse — and moving the mouse is the secret trigger.
6

A real person moved their mouse — and the trap sprang

The moment a real visitor moved their mouse (something a robot doesn't do), the hidden instructions woke up. They quietly downloaded the scrambled scam page, unlocked it with the password, and swapped it onto the screen — replacing the shop.

Why: the mouse movement is treated as proof "this is a real human, not a checker."
7

The victim was shown the fake "Microsoft Support" page

The screen filled with a fake Microsoft warning saying the computer was infected — with a fake command window and a scary voice message. It locked the screen, blocked the person from closing the tab, and told them to call a phone number urgently.

Why: panic and urgency push people to call before they can think it through.
8

The victim calls — and the scammers cash in

A frightened victim calls the number and reaches the scammers' fake "support" line, where the operators take payment for fake "help" and/or talk the victim into giving them remote access to the computer.

Why: this phone call is the whole point — it's where the scammers actually make their money.

The one trick that made it all work

"Robots don't move a mouse; people do." The whole scheme rests on that single idea. By waiting for a mouse movement before revealing anything, the scammers showed a clean page to Google's checkers and the real scam only to living human visitors. Everything else — the fake shop, the scrambling, the hidden server — exists to support that one trick.

How this kind of scam is stopped

In plain terms
  • Ordinary antivirus won't catch it — nothing is downloaded onto the computer; it's a trick, not a virus.
  • Testers can expose it by opening the page in a special browser that pretends to move the mouse — this makes the hidden scam reveal itself.
  • It gets shut down when people report the web link, the hidden server, and the phone number, so browsers add them to their block-lists and the hosting companies take them offline.
  • The catch: scammers throw away used links and make new ones quickly, so reporting and takedowns have to keep up.
